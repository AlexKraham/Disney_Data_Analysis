Walt Disney's Box Office Movie Data Analysis

By Alexander Phan

Founded in the early 1920s, The Walt Disney Company has released hundreds of movies in a wide range of genres and although directed towards a younger audience, their movies still captivate persons of all different ages.

We will go through the data analyze trends in Disney movies and how movies have changed over the decades.

On the right, you will see a general overview of all the movies and its total gross income received in dollars.

You can hover over any bubble to reveal more information in the form of a tooltip at any time.

To your right, you will see each movie clustered by genre of which there are 12 categories. In the data set, there are some missing values for the genre, these are encoded which the teal color as shown in the legend. The size of the bubble represents the proportion in terms of total gross income received from the movie. In this visualization, we can deduce at first glance that Disney has created many comedy and action movies. The action movies seem to generally garner an above-average total gross revenue.

Conversely, there seems to have been a lot of comedies and dramas released, but as shown the size of the bubbles are smaller.

Some questions that we might have right now, though, is how this gross income relates in terms of inflation. Also, how about the amount of time it takes for a certian movie to be produced? Intuitively, a drama or comedy movie might require much less resource to produce than an epic action movie. Unfortunately, this data set is limited and we may only be able to answer some of these questions.

In this bar chart, we can see that adventure clearly is the most popular genres that Disney releases. This category envelops many very high budget movies such as Star Wars VII Finding Nemo, The Jungle Book, Toy Story, and other classics.

However, we must take into account that most movies in the adventure category have a very high budget and require a lot of time to produce. For example, Star Wars VII grossed $940 million (adjusted for inflation) and the film's conception was back around 2012. On January 2013, they named directors. The script took about a year, and filming began in 2014. The film was released at the end of 2015. This was nearly a 3-4 year process.

If we contrast this with a comedy movie like Freaky Friday ($150 million, adjusted for inflation), the movie was wrapped up within about 1-2 years.

The question begs, which movie genre gives the higher rate of return and the fastest?
